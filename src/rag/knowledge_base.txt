A well-balanced exam should have 30% Easy, 40% Medium, and 30% Hard questions based on Bloom's Taxonomy.
Exams with more than 60% Hard questions cause student anxiety and reduce learning outcomes.
Every exam should test recall (Level 1), understanding (Level 2), and application (Level 3) at minimum.
Easy questions build student confidence and should never be completely absent.
Topic coverage should be distributed evenly to avoid over-testing one concept.
A single topic dominating 70%+ of an exam indicates poor curriculum alignment.
Bloom's Taxonomy has 6 levels: Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, Create.
Formative assessments should be easier than summative assessments.
Questions should be reviewed for cultural and linguistic bias regularly.
Low average scores (below 50%) suggest the exam difficulty is misaligned with student preparation.
Medium difficulty questions are the most diagnostic — they separate prepared from unprepared students.
Spiral questioning — revisiting topics at increasing difficulty — improves long-term retention.
Exams should never test content that was not explicitly taught in the course.
A mix of question types (MCQ, short answer, applied) tests deeper understanding than MCQs alone.
Students perform better when exams have a clear easy warm-up section at the start.
Difficulty should generally increase through the exam, not be randomly distributed.
Over-reliance on memorization questions reduces higher-order thinking development.
Assessment design should align with stated learning objectives (constructive alignment).
Peer-reviewed research shows spaced repetition improves retention by up to 80%.
An exam's reliability increases when it covers a broad range of topics with consistent difficulty spread.
